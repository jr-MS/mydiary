## The Time Machine — H. G. Wells (1895)

- Often credited with popularizing the modern “time machine” as a *device*—a piece of engineering you can build, ride, and describe.
- First published as a novel in 1895 (after earlier serialized versions), it helped define early science fiction as a way to test social ideas.
- The far-future world of the Eloi and Morlocks is a satirical mirror of Victorian class structure—comfort above, labor below.
- Wells treats time like a landscape: the Traveler “moves” through it, making the abstract feel mechanical and visual.
- The book’s bleak cosmic epilogue (the dying Earth) widens the story from social critique to deep-time humility.

今天读到（或重新想起）《时间机器》，我最喜欢的不是“穿越”本身，而是它把“进步”这件事硬生生拽回到审判台上：如果把时间拉长到足够远，所谓文明会不会只是把不平等打磨得更顺手、更隐蔽？Eloi 的轻盈与 Morlock 的阴影像一对被强行拆开的共同体——一个失去警觉、一个失去日光。它让我反省自己每天追求的效率、舒适与优化，究竟是在让生活更自由，还是在把某些代价推到看不见的地方。也许真正的“时间旅行”不是逃离当下，而是学会在当下看见长期后果，并对那些被我们便利所掩盖的人与事保持敏感。

What stays with me in *The Time Machine* isn’t the thrill of “going” to another era, but the way Wells drags the idea of progress into daylight and asks it to testify. Stretch time far enough and our proud narratives can collapse into a question: are we building a better world, or just polishing inequality until it becomes frictionless? The Eloi’s softness and the Morlocks’ darkness feel like one society split in two—one losing vigilance, the other losing sunlight. It makes me examine my own daily worship of efficiency and comfort: do these upgrades expand freedom, or do they simply relocate costs to places I don’t have to look? Maybe the real time travel is learning to see long consequences inside ordinary choices, and staying alert to whatever our convenience tries to hide.
