## 1) Title + author + year
**_The Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde_** — Robert Louis Stevenson, **1886**

## 2) Facts (3–6)
- The story is framed like a **mystery investigation**, with clues revealed through testimonies, documents, and late-arriving confessions.
- Stevenson wrote it in the context of **Victorian London**, where respectability and moral anxiety often coexisted with a thriving nightlife and rigid social codes.
- The novella popularized a lasting cultural shorthand: **“Jekyll and Hyde”** as a metaphor for divided character and hidden impulses.
- Early readers were struck by its blend of **Gothic horror and psychological speculation**, hinting at modern ideas about the self before Freud’s major works.
- It has inspired countless adaptations across theatre, film, comics, and games—often emphasizing different balances of **science, morality, and horror**.

## 3) Reflection (中文)
我喜欢这本书的地方，不只是“人有两面”这么简单的结论，而是它让人承认：自我并不是一块完整、透明的玻璃，而更像一间有很多门的屋子。我们常常把“体面”当作唯一的出口，把欲望与阴影锁进地下室，假装它们不在。但锁得越紧，反弹就越猛烈。与其把黑暗当成敌人，不如把它当作需要被看见、被管理的一部分——不是放纵，而是承认它的存在，给它边界、给自己选择。真正的成熟，也许不是永远光明，而是在光与暗之间还能保持清醒。

## 4) Reflection (English)
What stays with me isn’t the tidy takeaway that “people have two sides,” but the book’s insistence that the self is not a single, fully knowable unit. It’s closer to a house with locked rooms: we decorate the front hall with virtue and discipline, then pretend the basement doesn’t exist. Yet repression doesn’t erase anything—it concentrates it. The more we rely on appearances as our only identity, the more fragile that identity becomes. The harder work is integration: naming what we’re capable of, setting boundaries, and choosing deliberately rather than performing goodness. Growing up, in that sense, isn’t becoming purely bright—it’s remaining lucid while carrying both light and shadow.
